Learning guide

Step 1

Find resonance: move ω close to ωₙ and watch the amplitude increase.

Step 2

Add damping: increase c and observe how the resonance peak becomes lower and wider.

Step 3

Add nonlinearity: change α and compare phase portrait, spectrum, and Poincaré points.

Core formulas

Natural frequency:
ωₙ = √(k / m)

Damping ratio:
ζ = c / (2√(km))
Linear frequency response:
X(ω) = F₀ / √[(k − mω²)² + (cω)²]

Energy:
E = ½mv² + ½kx² + ¼αx⁴
1. Time response: displacement and velocity
The main plot. It shows how displacement and velocity change over time.
2. Acceleration response
Full-width acceleration plot, useful when comparing with accelerometer measurements.
3. Force balance
Shows external, restoring, damping, and inertia forces.
4. Phase portrait
A closed loop usually means periodic motion.
5. Energy exchange
Shows energy moving between kinetic and potential forms.
6. Displacement spectrum
Shows dominant frequencies and possible nonlinear harmonics.
7. Poincaré section
Samples once per forcing period. Useful for nonlinear behavior.
8. Linear amplitude–frequency response
Exact only when α = 0; reference curve when nonlinear stiffness is active.
9. Linear force transmissibility
Linear force transmissibility reference: transmitted force ratio versus frequency ratio.
10. Parameter sensitivity
Shows how amplitude changes when m, c, or k changes by ±20%.
11. Damping "c"omparison
Compares resonance curves for lower, current, and higher damping.
12. Bifurcation diagram
Samples displacement once per forcing period while sweeping excitation frequency. Useful for nonlinear Duffing-type behavior.
